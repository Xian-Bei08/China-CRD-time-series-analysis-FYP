Two forces pull in opposite directions

Indoor air pollution has been falling fast — and this trend is strongly associated in the time-series analysis with the improvement in respiratory health. But the population is ageing, and older people are more vulnerable. These two forces are working against each other.

Indoor air pollution (household PM) is falling fast

−90% in 33 years. Household PM exposure dropped from 283 to 29 µg/m³ — driven by cleaner cooking fuels, improved ventilation, and reduced solid fuel burning. This appears to be one of the most important factors associated with the observed decline in CRD burden.

Short-run fluctuations and long-run relationships

The data suggests that changes in household air pollution are not only associated with year-to-year variation in respiratory disease burden, but also exhibit a stable long-run level relationship over time. The ARDL(1,0,0) model — selected by information criterion — includes household air pollution and a time trend alongside the lagged dependent variable. These findings are based on a time-series modelling framework and should be interpreted as statistical associations rather than causal effects.

Evidence from time-series modelling

Using an ARDL-based framework (ARDL(1,0,0)), the analysis identifies a statistically significant long-run level relationship between household air pollution and CRD burden (bounds test F = 7.46, exceeding the 5% critical bound of 4.32). This implies that these variables move together over time in a systematic, non-random way.

What does this mean in practice?

The data points to clear priorities — and a warning about complacency.

The headline finding

Cleaner indoor air appears to be strongly associated with the long-term decline in respiratory disease burden — and is the key predictor identified by the modelling framework. But an ageing population is a growing structural pressure — and without continued progress on air quality, the gains could slow or reverse.

Where are we now vs 1990?
Key indicators — 1990 vs 2023
Progress is real but uneven. Disease burden and indoor pollution have improved dramatically. But ageing has accelerated and PM2.5 (outdoor) has barely changed — two factors that could limit future gains.
The tension: cleaner air vs more elderly people
Illustrative scenarios (not forecasts) — These scenarios are illustrative and based on observed associations, not formal forecasts.
The race between two trends: If household PM continues falling at recent rates, disease burden should keep declining. But if population ageing accelerates faster than air quality improves, the burden could stabilise or rise.
Three key takeaways
🏠
Indoor air quality is identified as a key associated factor
Household PM is the strongest statistical predictor of CRD burden. Policies promoting clean cooking fuels and improved ventilation have the highest expected payoff.
👴
Ageing is an emerging risk
The population aged 65+ has nearly tripled its share. Healthcare systems need to prepare for growing respiratory disease demand even as pollution falls.
⏱️
Statistical associations may operate with a lag
Household PM lags of one and two years are consistently selected as predictors in the modelling framework, suggesting that changes in exposure may take time to fully manifest in aggregate disease burden — though the precise lag magnitudes are uncertain at this sample size.
